## Page 3 — Mistake 01

**Mistake 01**
**Your homepage doesn't tell patients who you help in 5 seconds.**

**What it looks like:**
- Generic openers like "Welcome to XYZ Wellness"
- Soft-focus hero images of green leaves, pebbles, or hands holding tea
- Headlines about "holistic" care with no specific condition, patient, or outcome

**Why it costs you patients:**
A patient searching *"naturopath for gut issues Brisbane"* needs to see the words *gut* and *Brisbane* on your homepage to trust they've landed in the right place. If they don't, they close the tab. The average visitor decides whether to stay on a page in under 3 seconds. You're competing with that attention span.

**The fix:**
1. Replace your hero headline with a specific problem you solve or a specific patient you help
   *Example: "Functional medicine for women with chronic fatigue. Brisbane-based, Australia-wide via telehealth."*
2. Put your suburb or service area in your hero or sub-headline
3. Make your primary booking button visible without scrolling
4. Remove any stock photography that could live on any other practitioner's site

## Page 4 — Mistake 02

**Mistake 02**
**Your site is a wall of text. The eye has nowhere to land.**

**What it looks like:**
- Long paragraphs with no breaks
- No sub-headings to guide the reader
- Every section looks like the last — same size, same weight, same colour
- Important information buried in the third paragraph of a wall-of-text bio

**Why it costs you patients:**
Most website visitors don't read. They scan. If your page doesn't have clear visual hierarchy — headlines, sub-heads, bullet points, bolded takeaways — a patient with any time pressure (which is most of them) will bounce before they find the thing they came for.

This matters even more for patients who are already overwhelmed. Chronic illness, burnout, fatigue — the exact patients you serve — have less bandwidth for word vomit. Your site's job is to make the answer obvious.

**The fix:**
1. Break every section into three things: a headline, 1–2 sentences of context, and a clear next step
2. Use sub-headings every 100–150 words so the eye has somewhere to rest
3. Turn any list of three or more items into actual bullet points
4. Bold the single most important phrase in each paragraph
5. Add white space between sections — patients need room to breathe as much as they need information

## Page 5 — Mistake 03

**Mistake 03**
**Your services page is vague. No one knows what things cost or what's included.**

**What it looks like:**
- "Book a consultation to discuss pricing"
- Service descriptions that say *what* you do but not *how long, how much,* or *what's included*
- No mention of health fund rebates, telehealth options, or package deals
- A list of modalities ("iridology, herbal medicine, nutritional consulting") with no patient-facing context

**Why it costs you patients:**
Patients who have to email to ask the price don't email. They go to the next practitioner whose website tells them. Hidden pricing is the #1 reason patients abandon a practitioner's site before booking.

Transparency isn't just kindness — it's conversion. When a patient can see "90-minute initial consultation, $220, rebate eligible, in-person or telehealth," they have every piece of information they need to say yes. When they can't, they say "I'll look into it later." Which usually means never.

**The fix:**
1. List every service with its actual price — even if it's a "from $X" range
2. For each service, state: duration, format (in-person/telehealth), what's included, and what happens after
3. Display rebate logos (HICAPS, Medibank, BUPA, etc.) near the booking CTA
4. Include a "What to expect in your first consultation" section — format, prep, follow-up
5. If you offer packages, show them clearly. Don't force patients to book a call to find out

## Page 6 — Mistake 04

**Mistake 04**
**You have no clear call-to-action. No booking button. No lead magnet. No next step.**

**What it looks like:**
- A "Contact" link in the nav, and nothing else
- Booking link tucked into footer text
- No email list, no free guide, no way to capture interest from patients who aren't ready yet
- A homepage that ends in "Thank you for visiting" instead of pointing to the next action

**Why it costs you patients:**
Every website visitor is at a different stage of readiness. Maybe 5% are ready to book today. Another 20% are curious. The other 75% are researching, comparing, thinking about it.

If the only option on your website is "book a consultation," you capture 5% of visitors and lose the rest forever. A lead magnet — a free guide, checklist, or resource — captures the email addresses of the other 75%. Then you can nurture them into patients over weeks or months instead of hoping they remember you.

**The fix:**
1. **Primary CTA:** Book a consultation. Visible in the nav, in the hero, and repeated at least three times on the homepage.
2. **Secondary CTA:** A lead magnet tailored to your ideal patient. Examples: *"Gut Health Starter Guide," "3-Day Energy Reset Meal Plan," "What to Eat When You're Burnt Out."*
3. Every blog post, services page, and about page should end with a clear next step — not "thanks for reading"
4. Connect your lead magnet to an email platform so new subscribers get nurtured automatically
5. Review every page and ask: *"If this is the only page a patient sees, what do I want them to do next?"* If the answer isn't obvious, the page isn't done.

## Page 7 — Mistake 05

**Mistake 05**
**You're speaking clinical language when patients search in plain English.**

**What it looks like:**
- "Specialising in HPA-axis dysregulation and mitochondrial support"
- "Evidence-based functional medicine for complex chronic presentations"
- Service pages full of modality names, not patient problems
- Service titles like "Iridology" instead of "Find the root cause of your fatigue"

**Why it costs you patients:**
Patients don't search for *"HPA-axis dysregulation."* They search *"always tired for no reason."* Google's algorithm matches search intent to page content — if your page doesn't contain the words real patients type, you don't appear in the results.

Even when they do land, patients don't recognise their own problem in clinical terminology. They came looking for someone who understands their fatigue, their bloating, their anxiety. If your copy reads like a peer-reviewed paper, they assume you're not talking to them.

**The fix:**
1. Write a list of the top 10 things patients actually say when they book
   *"I'm tired all the time." "My gut feels off." "My hormones are out of whack." "I can't sleep."*
2. Rewrite your homepage and services pages using those phrases first — clinical terminology second, as the *"how I treat it"*
3. Create service pages for patient problems, not modalities. *"Fatigue"* beats *"Functional Medicine"* for SEO and conversion
4. Keep your About page professional and clinical — that's where your credentials belong. Everywhere else, write for patients

## Design Notes

**Palette:**
- Primary: Sage Dark #4a6e57
- Accent: Sage #7a9e87 and Sage Light #b8d4c0
- Background: Cream #faf8f4
- Text: Ink Charcoal #2a2520

**Typography:**
- Headers: Cormorant Garamond (match website)
- Body: Inter or Public Sans (clean, readable)
- Mistake numerals: Large (80–120pt), sage, outline or light-weight

**Layout principles:**
- One idea per page — resist cramming
- Generous white space — show, don't tell the "don't be a wall of text" rule
- Left-align body copy, centred headlines for mistake titles
- Use pull quotes or callout boxes for the "why it costs you patients" section
- CTA button on pages 2, 8, and 9 at minimum
- Consistent footer on every page: logo + page number + website

**What NOT to include:**
- Stock photography of wellness clichés
- Cluttered design
- More than 2 fonts
- Emojis
- Any of the banned words: transform, unlock, skyrocket, holistic, seamless, journey, game-changer
